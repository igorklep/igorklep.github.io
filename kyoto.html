Kyoto 5 lectures (2024)

Free Noncommutative Real Algebraic Geometry and Analysis: Selected Topics

I. Positive Noncommutative Polynomials

Main topic of Day 1 was Helton's sum of squares theorem with the elementary convexity inspired proof. An alternate proof inspired by complete positivity is due to McCullough and can be found at https://doi.org/10.1016/S0024-3795(00)00285-8. It is Theorem 0.2, and the proof in Section 4 needs Section 2 on noncommutative Hankel matrices.

Board pictures of Day 1

Day 2 proved the Helton-McCullough archimedean Positivstellensatz, followed by an excursion into commutative real algebraic geometry. We discussed Putinar's Positivstellensatz and Hilbert's 17th problem. Lastly, we touched upon applications of noncommutative real algebraic geometry to quantum physics via the Navascues-Pironio-Acin (NPA) hierarchy and Bell inequalities.

Additional reading to go beyond the material covered in class

We skipped chapter II. Noncommutative Nullstellensätze. It would have covered zeros of noncommutative polynomials, including geometry of their vanishing sets, connections to factorization and a Nullstellensatz. And perhaps the solution to the 1985 Curto-Herrero and 2003 Hadwin-Larson conjecture.

III. Noncommutative Convexity

Day 3 introduced (free) spectrahedra and matrix convex sets and proved the Effros-Winkler Hahn-Banach separation theorem.

Board pictures of Day 3

Here is a spiced up version of the 3rd puzzle of the day. Suppose \(d\) is given and you can choose \(s,t\in\mathbb N_0\) such that \(d=s+t\). Then you are given a possibly biased coin with probability for head \(\frac sd\).

Now you toss the coin \(d\) times.

If you obtain at least \(s\) times head, you pay me \(t\) Euros. If you obtain at least \(t\) times tail, you pay me \(s\) Euros. (Consequently, if you obtain exactly \(s\) times head, then you pay \(d\) Euros in total.)

Which coin should you choose to minimize the expected loss?

Day 4 explained the Webster-Winkler duality between compact matrix convex sets and operator systems, polars and spectrahedrops, LMI or spectrahedral inclusion, we discussed the Convex Positivstellensatz, the matrix cube problem of Nemirovskii and its solution with the Simultaneous Dilation Theory.

We didn't talk about extreme points or computing matrix convex hulls via spectrahedrops.

IV. Noncommutative Function Theory

Day 5 talked about noncommutative functions and sets, proved that continuous implies analytic, introduced noncommutative rational functions and their linear realizations. Finally, we saw the Grothendieck-Ax theorem, the Jacobian conjecture and their noncommutative counterparts.

Homework

Deadline: 31 July 2024

Pick one of the first two problems (#2 is easier), do problem #3, pick one of the problems #4, #5 (#4 is slightly harder), and one of the problems #6, #7.

  1. Let \(f=f^*\in {\mathbb R}\langle x_1,\ldots, x_d\rangle\) be a noncommutative polynomial of degree \(\leq 2\delta\). Consider the set of all palindromic words appearing in \(f\), and stack all their right-hand side chips of length \(\leq\delta\) into a vector \(V\). (For example, \(x_1x_3x_1x_3x_1\) is palindromic, and if \(\delta=3\), we would extract \(x_1,x_3x_1,x_1x_3x_1\).) Prove that \(f\) is a sum of squares if and only if there is a positive semidefinite matrix \(G\) with \(f=V^*GV\).

  2. Show that the block matrix \(\begin{bmatrix} A & B \\ B^* & 0\end{bmatrix}\) is positive semidefinite if and only if \(A\) is positive semidefinite and \(B=0\).

  3. Consider the linear pencil \(L(x,y)=\begin{bmatrix} 1&x&y \\ x& 1 &0\\ y & 0 & 1\end{bmatrix}\). Show that the associated free spectrahedron \(\mathcal D_L\) is equal to \(\mathcal D_L=\bigcup_{n\in\mathbb N}\{ (X,Y)\in M_n(\mathbb R)^2_{\rm sa} | 1-X^2-Y^2 \succeq 0 \}.\)
    Hint: If \(A\) is invertible, then \( \begin{bmatrix} A & B\\ C & D\end{bmatrix}= \begin{bmatrix} A & B\\ C & D\end{bmatrix}= \begin{bmatrix} I & 0 \\ CA^{-1} & I \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} A & 0 \\ 0 & D-CA^{-1}B \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} I & A^{-1}B \\ 0 & I \end{bmatrix}\).

  4. Consider the noncommutative version of the unit \(4\)-disk (sometimes also called the TV screen) \(\mathcal D_p\) for \(p=1-x^4-y^4\). Show that \(\mathcal D_p(1)\) is convex, but \(\mathcal D_p(2)\) is not.

  5. Let us call a noncommutative function f nc convex if for all self-adjoint matrices \(A,B\) (of all sizes), \(f\Big(\frac 12(A+B)\Big) \preceq \frac12 \big(f(A)+f(B)\big)\). Show that \(x^2\) is nc convex, but \(x^4\) is not.

  6. Consider the noncommutative rational function \(r(x,y):=(xy-yx)^{-1}\). Find a linear realization for \(r\). (Beware, it will not be monic.) What is the smallest size realization you can find?

  7. Let \(\mathscr U\) be a noncommutative set and \(f\) a noncommutative function defined on \(\mathscr U\).

    1. Show that the similarity closure \(\hat {\mathscr U}\) of \(\mathscr U\), i.e., the smallest noncommutative set that contains \(\mathscr U\) and is closed under conjugation, is equal to \( \hat {\mathscr U} = \{ P^{-1} (X_1 \oplus \cdots \oplus X_r) P \mid r\in \mathbb N,\, X_i \in \mathscr U,\, P \text{ invertible}\}.\)

    2. Prove that \(f\) extends to a noncommutative function on \(\hat{ \mathscr U}\).
